Hydrogen Fuel Cells

The most common type of hydrogen fuel cell is called a polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) fuel cell. A PEM cell works by sandwiching an electrolyte membrane between an anode and a cathode. Positively-charged hydrogen is introduced to the anode and negatively-charged oxygen, from air, to the cathode. Protons travel through the membrane from the anode to the cathode, producing an electrical current that can then be utilized ("Fuel Cell"). In the case of a hydrogen fuel cell, the positive ions are made of hydrogen and the negative ions are made of oxygen ("Use of Hydrogen"):

Electrolyte Membrane

In the diagram above, the electrolyte membrane is in the center layer, between where the oxygen and hydrogen are introduced into the cell. According to Grujicic and Chittajallu, the chemical structure consists of three regions:
  1. A fluorocarbon backbone with hundreds of repeating \(-\text{C}\text{F}_2-\text{C}\text{F}-\text{C}\text{F}_2-\) units.
  2. \(-\text{O}-\text{C}\text{F}_2-\text{C}\text{F}-\text{O}-\text{C}\text{F}_2-\text{C}\text{F}_2-\) side chains which connect the backbone to the third region.
  3. Ionic clusters of sulfonic acid ions (\(\text{S}\text{O}_3^{-} \text{ H}^{+}\)). The \(\text{S}\text{O}_3^{-}\) ions attach to the side chains (region 2) and stay immobile; however, when water is introduced, the \(\text{H}^{+}\) ions form hydronium ions (\(\text{H}_3\text{O}^{+}\)) and easily hop between \(\text{S}\text{O}_3^{-}\) sites in the membrane, allowing the PEM to conduct hydrogen ions (Grujicic and Chittajallu 57).

Producing hydrogen

To isolate the hydrogen for the fuel cell, a process known as electrolysis is commonly used ("Hydrogen Production"). According to ("23.9 Electrolysis"), the electrolytic cell contains two platinum electrodes in water containing a small amount of electrolyte (e.g. \(\text{H}_2\text{S}\text{O}_4\)) to carry charge. Electrolysis would look like the following, at the anode and cathode, respectively: $$\begin{align*}2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow \text{O}_2(g) + 4\text{H}^{+}(aq) + 4e^{-} \quad E^0 &= -1.23 \text{ V}\\ 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) + 2e^{-} \rightarrow \text{H}_2(g) + 2\text{O}\text{H}^{-}(aq) \quad E^0 &= -0.83 \text{ V}.\end{align*}$$ The overall reaction is then \(2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow \text{O}_2(g)\) with a value of \(E_{\text{cell}}^0 = -2.06 \text{ V}\). Platinum is commonly used because it is relatively inert, so it will not give away or attempt to steal electrons in one of the redox reactions; however, platinum and other noble metals are expensive, which is a disadvantage for the process (Nasser et al.).

Benefits

  • Hydrogen energy is environmentally friendly because a hydrogen fuel cell only emits \(\text{H}_2\) and \(\text{O}\) (Hosseini and Butler 13).
  • By weight, has an energy yield 2.75 times greater than hydrocarbon fuels (Nasser et al.).
  • Can be integrated into variable/intermittent renewable power generators such as wind farms, allowing flexibility and less new infrastructure needed ("Hydrogen Production").

Limitations

  • Current production methods cost more than double that of diesel and petrol and are not commercially viable (Hosseini and Butler 14).
  • Using current grid energy to produce hydrogen would result in greenhouse emissions depending on your location, which just shifts the problem down the line ("Hydrogen Production").
